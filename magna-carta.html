Magna Carta Copies

the distribution of power

background

The Magna Carta, while not on the World Heritage List, is included in the UNESCO Memory of the World International Register, the document equivalent of the list as mentioned earlier. UNESCO writes, “No other document has left its imprint on the development of human rights as the Magna Carta.” [1] This quarter, we were fortunate enough to have visited the locations of three of the original four Magna Carta copies at the British Library and Salisbury Cathedral. One of the British Library copies was damaged in a fire in 1731 and while it was still legible after the fire, attempts to restore it later made it illegible. A legend about the other British Library copy is that it was rescued from being cut up for collar backing. The Salisbury Cathedral copy is doubted to be an authentic copy as it contains several clerical errors more than the others and its handwriting style differs in addition to its disappearance from around 1700 to 1814. However, the consensus still accepts it as one of the official 1215 copies. [2] Yes, it was made in the year 1215, which prompts the question, why do we keep these old pieces of paper around?

research

The Magna Carta was the result of a baronial rebellion against King John, and it, “established the principle that all free men have the right to justice and a fair trial, and that no one, not even the King, is above the law.” Although it can be seen as a failed document as civil war broke out following the document’s creation, prompting charters to be made in 1216, 1217, 1225, and 1297, it is still one of if not the first document that shows a division of power between the ruling government, here the monarchy, and the people. It not only gave power to the people but it also gave more freedom to the English Church as outlined in the first chapter. Before the Magna Carta, clergy travel to Rome was restricted by the monarchy to limit the pope’s power over English affairs.

Since its inception, the Magna Carta has inspired many documents such as the US Bill of Rights, the English Bill of Rights, and the UN’s Universal Declaration of Human Rights; however, many of its articles are now defunct and its interpretations vary from person to person. There are two major views on its interpretation: one view looks at the document only in the context of its feudal origin while the other looks at the document as the result of visionaries of their time. This reminds me of the different views of the US Constitution, mainly originalism and the living document view respectively, which makes sense as it was fashioned after the Magna Carta.

conclusion

Aside from the Magna Carta being one of the first examples of a founding document, it also represents one of the first instances in which power is divided from the government to the people. It seems to me that we keep these documents because they have changed from a ruling document to one that is not necessarily in use aside from a few clauses, but it represents the power of the people. Similar to Blenheim Palace, I believe that it represents the feeling of being heard.
