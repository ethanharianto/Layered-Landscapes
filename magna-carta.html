Magna Carta Copies

the distribution of power

background

The Magna Carta, while not on the World Heritage List, is included in the UNESCO Memory of the World International Register, the document equivalent of the list as mentioned earlier. UNESCO writes, “No other document has left its imprint on the development of human rights as the Magna Carta.” This quarter, we were fortunate enough to have visited the locations of three of the original four Magna Carta copies at the British Library and Salisbury Cathedral. One of the British Library copies was damaged in a fire in 1731 and while it was still legible after the fire, attempts to restore it later made it illegible. A legend about the other British Library copy is that it was rescued from being cut up for collar backing. The Salisbury Cathedral copy is doubted to be an authentic copy as it contains several clerical errors more than the others and its handwriting style differs in addition to its disappearance from around 1700 to 1814. However, the consensus still accepts it as one of the official 1215 copies. Yes, it was made in the year 1215, which prompts the question, why do we keep these old pieces of paper around?
