Cecil Rhodes Statue

ties to a colonial past

background

Cecil Rhodes is a 19th-century figure who was prominent in the history of British colonialism, especially in Southern Africa. He was a student at Oxford's Oriel College from 1873 to 1881. He then became a major donor to the College after he died in 1902, in which he donated £100,000 to the college, which equates to a sum of £12 million today. £40,000 of his donation was used to construct the High Street building which houses his statue. [1] There was a similar statue installed at the University of Cape Town in 1934; however, after the Rhodes Must Fall Movement in 2015, it was taken down. Similar movements have unfolded regarding the Oriel installment, but only to the result of a new plaque providing context to Rhodes’ actions.[2]

research

In the background that I provided, it may seem ridiculous why a statue may be so controversial. That is because I did not provide the context that Rhodes was at the forefront of the violent British takeover of southern Africa, mirroring how Oriel College let the statue stand for over a century with no context whatsoever. Zofia Stemplowska argues in her paper why the statue should be removed against three arguments in support of Oriel’s actions. She first argues against the morality of continuing to display the statue. She concludes this section by acknowledging bringing the statue down is unfamiliar territory as it stands as some sort of backtracking. I would think that this morality argument is the first argument that people jump to when attacking the statue because it is true that it is deplorable to honor a man who has done such heinous things; on the other hand, the Rhodes Scholarship gives exceptional students a chance to study at Oxford and that is built from Cecil Rhodes’ money, so it is hard to find the balance of what to honor.

It reminds me of the musicians of recent times who did questionable things, but their music is separate from them. For example, Kanye West is controversial in the things he has said recently, but he is still regarded as one of the greatest artists of the generation. Stemplowska then takes on several arguments that are quite comical such as turning the statue to face the corner, similar to a kindergartner in timeout. However, I find her conclusion compelling. She writes, “If Oriel does nothing in anticipation of a ruling that keeps the statue in place, they are not depriving the government of opportunities to display its accommodating attitude to racism, but accommodating it themselves.” This reminds me of the situation of David Starr Jordan at Stanford.[3] Freshmen are now required to take COLLEGE101 in which one of the subject matters is Jordan and his statues were taken down. In contrast to Oriel, Stanford removed the statues and is giving further context to its students about Jordan’s wrongdoings.

conclusion

What does this all mean in the context of the original question? The Rhodes statue once stood for a sort of nationalism, an integral part of a nation’s cultural heritage and identity. However, over time, with the context of his actions, it grew to be a symbol of racism and a regrettable imperial past. Ultimately, the evolving perception of the Rhodes statue reflects a societal shift towards a more nuanced understanding of English history. Confronting uncomfortable truths is essential for shaping a more just future. Failure to do so risks perpetuating harmful narratives and erasing the collective heritage. The debate prompts reflection on how to confront the past, recognizing that honest acknowledgment is vital for progress.
