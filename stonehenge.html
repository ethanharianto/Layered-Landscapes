Stonehenge and Avebury

preservation of a mystery

background

Stonehenge and Avebury, like Blenheim Palace, have been declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site [1], fulfilling criteria (i) and (iii): “to represent a masterpiece of human creative genius,” and, “to bear a unique or at least exceptional testimony to a cultural tradition or to a civilization which is living or which has disappeared.”[2] It also fulfills criteria (ii) as Blenheim does. We do not know, for sure, what these stone circles were used for, but the UNESCO site believes they were meant for ceremonial and mortuary use. Stonehenge is impressive in the size of the stones used to construct its circle while Avebury’s stone circle is the largest in circumference; both are dated to between 3700 and 1600 BC which further adds to their mystery. However, today, Stonehenge is the one we learn about in school and is much more of an attraction comparatively despite Avebury having just as much of an allure, how did that happen?

research

During my research, I stumbled upon a book by Amy Gazin-Schwartz called Marketing Heritage: Archaeology and the Consumption of the Past, more importantly, a chapter called, “Mementos of the Past: Material Culture of Tourism at Stonehenge and Avebury.” Unlike the past few destinations, we do not know what Stonehenge and Avebury were initially meant for, but we do know how we have treated them in the past few millennia, particularly in the past few decades for the use of tourism. Gazin-Schwartz examines the role that a gift shop plays in connecting tourists to the sites they visit and concludes that while it is more inviting to the average tourist, it detracts from what makes Stonehenge so special. She quotes archaeologist Christopher Tillery, “It’s always much larger in the imagination than it is on the ground.” This is a sentiment that I believe most of the class felt and Professor Treharne also shares. It’s a bit unfortunate because Stonehenge should be a special place that people visit, but it has been changed to something on the same level as an amusement park, to be honest. This is further evidenced by the English Heritage website for Stonehenge in which they advertise private tours and their different exhibitions.

Avebury, on the other hand, is much less capitalized in that it is both a village and part of an intricate stone circle. Gazin-Schwartz describes the village and stone circle as nowhere near as welcoming as Stonehenge but in contrast to Stonehenge, the stone circle exists as part of their everyday life. While the Stonehenge gift shop contains trinkets that are specific to Stonehenge, Avebury’s contains quite mundane British trinkets with some Avebury postcards. However, both gift shops allow tourists to connect to history through whatever media they choose whether it be a postcard, a sweatshirt, or a rock.[3],

conclusion

Answering the question above, Stonehenge is much more of an attraction while Avebury is integrated into the village and is hard to spot as a World Heritage Site. However, going back to the original research question, we do not know how these places originated; however, they have changed to become tourist traps like Blenheim Palace. On the other hand, it does not signify a connection to the people like Blenheim Palace and the Magna Carta; it does so by allowing the tourists to interpret the sites for themselves, offering both sides of the coin in these two destinations, which gives the people a different kind of power, the power of imagination and choice.
