Blenheim Palace

an imperial staple

background

Blenheim Palace is not only a site important to English Heritage, but UNESCO also deemed it a World Heritage Site. Although it is widely attributed to Sir Winston Churchill today, it was given to the First Duke of Marlborough, John Churchill, after his victory over French and Bavarian troops in 1704, which is said to have decided the future of the British Empire. It fulfills criteria (ii) and (iv) for selection onto the world heritage list: “to exhibit an important interchange of human values, over a span of time or within a cultural area of the world, on developments in architecture or technology, monumental arts, town-planning or landscape design;” and, “to be an outstanding example of a type of building, architectural or technological ensemble or landscape which illustrates (a) significant stage(s) in human history.” Unlike the Rhodes statue, Blenheim Palace does not have a shroud of controversy, but it has evolved from a residence of an English aristocracy to, when I visited, a place centered around one of England’s favorite leaders, Sir Winston Churchill. Why is that?

research

As I mentioned before, the palace was meant to be a reward to the Duke of Marlborough for a victory, which may have brought about the end of the War of the Spanish Succession; however, at the time of his death, it was not even finished. While the Palace is not controversial now, it used to be. Samuel J. Rogal puts it simply, “The basis for all controversy and difficulty surrounding the building of Blenheim Palace was, simply, money.” The architect, John Vanbrugh, had originally had a cost of £40,000 in mind, but that ballooned up to no less than £325,000 by the palace’s completion. Now since the Duke had already passed, who was handing over the money? It was none other than his wife, Sarah Churchill, who did so quite annoyed as she believed Vanbrugh to be deceitful in his costs. I believe this shows the pomposity of the English aristocracy because although the cost continued to rise, she decided the palace still needed to be done. The dukes that succeeded John Churchill then continued to pour more money into the palace for smoking rooms, painting exhibits, china collections, and repairs.

In 1712, visitors were allowed to be shown around by the comptroller for free. In the mid-18th century, Oxford tours began with fees that were regarded as exorbitant. In the later 18th century, opening hours were restricted to certain hours in the afternoon with a few public days in the summer. In the 19th century, the duke at the time then allowed shooting and fishing by the hour; however, his successor raised the entry fee which was followed by public outrage. In the late 19th century, the family began to live more in the palace (I have no idea why they would not want to reside there the whole year) so public access was further restricted. This all changed in the First World War, also known as the Great War, in which the long library was turned into a hospital ward. The palace was then used again in the Second World War by the Intelligence Service, the British Council, and the Ministry of Supply. After the war, the palace was restored and its doors opened once again to the public.

conclusion

We have answered my original question of how the palace turned from a residence of aristocracy to a tourist trap. However, what does this mean in terms of the overarching research question? From what we learned, this place of cultural heritage has changed. It used to be an exclusive place where even notable visitors were not given the time of day, and now anyone can go in (if they can afford to go in). What does that mean for its meaning? Well, it used to represent exclusivity, aristocracy, and loosely imperialism. However, it seems to now represent the government and the transfer of power thereof. While there are still countless aristocratic artifacts, much of the palace exhibits are centered around Sir Winston Churchill. How does that now contribute to the English identity? The transfer of power from the aristocracy to the popular government allowed the common voice that was heard to change from being a noble to truly the common person. As Arlette Farge described, back in the day, the common people were not heard because the nobles were taking up all the air time. And in a way, I believe Winston Churchill represents this feeling.
